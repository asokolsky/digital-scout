# Investment Accounts

An account wrapper controls:

- how money enters the account,
- how distributions are taxed,
- which investments are available, and
- whether required minimum distributions apply.

The wrapper is separate from the investment held inside it.

## Traditional 401(k)

- Contributions: usually pre-tax salary deferrals.
- Employer match: sometimes, depending on the employer plan.
- Investment menu: limited to the plan menu.
- Early distributions: generally taxable and may face a 10% additional tax; the
  age-55 separation-from-service exception can apply to qualified plans, but not
  IRAs.
- RMDs: generally required while the owner is alive; some plans allow delay
  until retirement.
- Main caveat: plan fees and investment menus vary; employer match can outweigh
  limits.

## Roth 401(k)

- Contributions: after-tax salary deferrals.
- Employer match: sometimes; plan rules govern employer money.
- Investment menu: limited to the plan menu.
- Early distributions: uses the qualified-plan early distribution framework,
  including the 10% additional tax framework unless an exception applies.
- RMDs: no lifetime RMDs for designated Roth 401(k) and 403(b) owners under
  current IRS RMD guidance. Beneficiaries are still subject to RMD rules.
- Main caveat: qualified Roth distributions can be tax-free, but five-year and
  early-distribution rules still determine how earnings are taxed.

## Traditional or Rollover IRA

- Contributions: may be deductible; rollovers usually preserve tax type.
- Employer match: none.
- Investment menu: broad, subject to IRS and custodian restrictions.
- Early distributions: before age 59 1/2, generally taxable and may face a 10%
  additional tax unless an exception applies.
- RMDs: generally required at age 73 under current IRS guidance.
- Main caveat: deductibility depends on income, filing status, and workplace
  coverage.
- Rollover note: a rollover IRA usually inherits the tax character of the account
  rolled into it.

## Roth IRA

- Contributions: after-tax and not deductible.
- Employer match: none.
- Investment menu: broad, subject to IRS and custodian restrictions.
- Early distributions: qualified distributions are tax-free; other withdrawals
  depend on Roth ordering and qualification rules.
- RMDs: no lifetime RMDs for the original owner.
- Main caveat: MAGI phaseouts and five-year/qualified-distribution rules matter.

## Taxable Brokerage Account

- Contributions: after-tax money.
- Employer match: none.
- Investment menu: broad, including stocks, ETFs, mutual funds, bonds, and
  others.
- Early distributions: no retirement-account early distribution penalty.
- RMDs: none.
- Main caveat: dividends, interest, and realized capital gains/losses are
  taxable.

## Quick Takeaways

- Employer matching can make a 401(k) attractive even when its investment menu
  is narrower.
- IRAs usually offer a broader investment menu, but some investments are not
  allowed and custodians can still restrict choices.
- Roth accounts trade current taxes for possible tax-free qualified
  distributions later.
- Taxable brokerage accounts are flexible, but realized gains and investment
  income can create annual tax reporting.

## Roth Conversion

A Roth conversion moves money from traditional retirement treatment into Roth
treatment. In practice, that usually means recognizing taxable income now in
exchange for possible tax-free qualified Roth distributions later.

Pros:

- Can reduce future lifetime RMD exposure by moving assets from traditional
  retirement accounts into Roth accounts.
- Can improve tax diversification by splitting future retirement income across
  taxable, tax-deferred, and Roth sources.
- Can support estate planning when heirs would benefit from inheriting Roth
  assets rather than fully taxable traditional retirement assets.

Cons:

- Conversion income can increase the current year's taxable income.
- Higher income from conversions may increase Medicare Part B and Part D premiums
  through IRMAA.
- Timing matters: a large conversion can push income into a higher tax bracket or
  affect other income-sensitive tax items.
- Reporting matters: IRS Form 8606 is used for conversions from traditional,
  SEP, or SIMPLE IRAs to Roth IRAs.

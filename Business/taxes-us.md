# US Taxes

## Filing Status

[IRS Interactive Tax Assistant](https://www.irs.gov/help/ita/what-is-my-filing-status):

Filing status affects filing requirements, standard deduction, eligibility for
certain credits, and the correct tax. If more than one status applies, compare
the allowed options.

- Single: unmarried, divorced, or legally separated taxpayers who do not qualify for another status.
- Married Filing Jointly: married couples combining their incomes and deductions on a single return. This often results in lower tax than filing separately, but compare if both statuses are available.
- Married Filing Separately: married couples who choose to file individual returns. This may result in lower taxes for some individuals but disqualifies you from certain credits.
- Head of Household: for unmarried or considered-unmarried taxpayers who pay more than half the cost of keeping up a home for a qualifying person, e.g. a child or qualifying parent.
- Qualifying Surviving Spouse: widowed taxpayers with a dependent child. This status maintains the lower tax rates of Married Filing Jointly for up to two years after the spouse's death.

## Terminology

- Total Income on Form 1040 line 9: all taxable income before adjustments.
- [Adjusted Gross Income](https://taxfoundation.org/taxedu/glossary/adjusted-gross-income-agi/) = Total Income - [above-the-line deductions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Above-the-line_deduction).
  AGI is reported on Form 1040 line 11.
- [Modified Adjusted Gross Income](https://www.irs.gov/credits-deductions/modified-adjusted-gross-income) = AGI plus specific add-backs.
  MAGI is not a single universal number: calculate it for the specific credit,
  deduction, tax, or account rule you are testing.
- Taxable Income = AGI - deductions
- Deductions - reduce your TI, the effect depends on your marginal tax rate.
- Tax Credits - reduce your tax bill dollar-for-dollar.

## Deductions, Standard vs Itemized

The standard deduction is a fixed deduction amount based mostly on filing status
and tax year. Itemized deductions are specific deductible expenses. Use whichever
gives the larger deduction.

Common itemized deductions:

- State and Local Taxes (SALT): property taxes, state income and sales taxes.
  Under current law, the cap is $40,000 for 2025, $40,400 for 2026, 1% higher
  each year through 2029, and $10,000 after 2029. For Married Filing Separately,
  use half the cap. Before 2030, the higher cap phases down by MAGI, but not
  below $10,000.
- Mortgage interest, generally on up to $750k of acquisition debt for newer mortgages
- Charitable contributions
- Medical expenses, if > 7.5% AGI

Bunching strategy: if close to itemizing threshold, combine deductions in one year.

## Tax Deductions vs Tax Credits

### Tax Deductions

Deductions reduce your taxable income, hence the effect depends on your marginal tax rate.

### Tax Credits

Credits reduce your tax bill dollar-for-dollar.

Nonrefundable Credits: can reduce tax to zero, excess is lost, e.g. child and dependent care credit, Lifetime learning credit.

Refundable Credits: create a refund beyond taxes paid, e.g. earned income credit.

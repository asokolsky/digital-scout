# US Taxes

## Filing Status

[IRS Interactive Tax Assistant](https://www.irs.gov/help/ita/what-is-my-filing-status):

Filing status affects filing requirements, standard deduction, eligibility for
certain credits, and the correct tax. If more than one status applies, compare
the allowed options.

- Single: unmarried, divorced, or legally separated taxpayers who do not qualify for another status.
- Married Filing Jointly: married couples combining their incomes and deductions on a single return. This often results in lower tax than filing separately, but compare if both statuses are available.
- Married Filing Separately: married couples who choose to file individual returns. This may result in lower taxes for some individuals but disqualifies you from certain credits.
- Head of Household: for unmarried or considered-unmarried taxpayers who pay more than half the cost of keeping up a home for a qualifying person, e.g. a child or qualifying parent.
- Qualifying Surviving Spouse: widowed taxpayers with a dependent child. This status maintains the lower tax rates of Married Filing Jointly for up to two years after the spouse's death.

## Terminology

- Total Income on Form 1040 line 9: all taxable income before adjustments.
- [Adjusted Gross Income](https://taxfoundation.org/taxedu/glossary/adjusted-gross-income-agi/) = Total Income - [above-the-line deductions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Above-the-line_deduction).
  AGI is reported on Form 1040 line 11.
- [Modified Adjusted Gross Income](https://www.irs.gov/credits-deductions/modified-adjusted-gross-income) = AGI plus specific add-backs.
  MAGI is not a single universal number: calculate it for the specific credit,
  deduction, tax, or account rule you are testing.
- Taxable Income = AGI - deductions
- Deductions - reduce your TI, the effect depends on your marginal tax rate.
- Tax Credits - reduce your tax bill dollar-for-dollar.

IRS on

- [AGI](https://www.irs.gov/filing/adjusted-gross-income)
- [MAGI](https://www.irs.gov/credits-deductions/modified-adjusted-gross-income)

See, e.g. [The 2026 Guide To Paying Less In Taxes](https://guide.sillymoney.com/).

| Income                                         | Description                                                       | Example                                                     |
| ---------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------- |
| Total Income                                   | wages + interest + dividends + rentals + etc, on Form 1040 line 9 | [$71,000](https://www.irs.gov/filing/adjusted-gross-income) |
| AGI = Total Income - above-the-line deductions | adjustments such as educator expenses or student loan interest    | [$68,250](https://www.irs.gov/filing/adjusted-gross-income) |
| MAGI = AGI + rule-specific add-backs           | add-backs depend on the tax benefit or tax rule being tested      | $69,000                                                     |
| TI = AGI - deductions                          | standard or itemized deductions                                   | $40,000                                                     |

## Deductions, Standard vs Itemized

The standard deduction is a fixed deduction amount based mostly on filing status
and tax year. Itemized deductions are specific deductible expenses. Use whichever
gives the larger deduction.

Common itemized deductions:

- State and Local Taxes (SALT): property taxes, state income and sales taxes.
  Under current law, the cap is $40,000 for 2025, $40,400 for 2026, 1% higher
  each year through 2029, and $10,000 after 2029. For Married Filing Separately,
  use half the cap. Before 2030, the higher cap phases down by MAGI, but not
  below $10,000.
- Mortgage interest, generally on up to $750k of acquisition debt for newer mortgages
- Charitable contributions
- Medical expenses, if > 7.5% AGI

Bunching strategy: if close to itemizing threshold, combine deductions in one year.

## Tax Deductions vs Tax Credits

### Tax Deductions

Deductions reduce your taxable income, hence the effect depends on your marginal tax rate.

### Tax Credits

Credits reduce your tax bill dollar-for-dollar.

Nonrefundable Credits: can reduce tax to zero, excess is lost, e.g. child and dependent care credit, Lifetime learning credit.

Refundable Credits: create a refund beyond taxes paid, e.g. earned income credit.

## Ordinary Income Tax Brackets

For the [2026 tax year](https://www.irs.gov/newsroom/irs-releases-tax-inflation-adjustments-for-tax-year-2026-including-amendments-from-the-one-big-beautiful-bill),
single filers use these federal brackets:

| Tax rate | Taxable income layer |
| -------- | -------------------- |
| 10%      | $0 to $12,400        |
| 12%      | Over $12,400         |
| 22%      | Over $50,400         |
| 24%      | Over $105,700        |
| 32%      | Over $201,775        |
| 35%      | Over $256,225        |
| 37%      | Over $640,600        |

Example for a single filer with $60,000 of taxable income in 2026:

- first $12,400 taxed at 10% = $1,240
- next $38,000, from $12,400 to $50,400, taxed at 12% = $4,560
- remaining $9,600, from $50,400 to $60,000, taxed at 22% = $2,112
- total federal ordinary income tax before credits = $7,912

The marginal rate is 22%, because the next dollar would be taxed at 22%. The
effective rate on ordinary taxable income is about 13.2%, because $7,912 /
$60,000 = 13.19%.

## Capital Gains, Short and Long Term

[IRS Topic 409](https://www.irs.gov/taxtopics/tc409) defines a capital gain or
loss as the difference between the amount realized on sale and adjusted basis.
For most investments, adjusted basis starts with what you paid, then changes for
items such as reinvested dividends, return of capital, improvements, or prior
depreciation.

The holding period decides whether the gain is short term or long term:

- Short-term capital gain or loss: asset held for one year or less. Net
  short-term capital gains are taxed as ordinary income at graduated tax rates.
- Long-term capital gain or loss: asset held for more than one year. Net
  long-term capital gains generally use the preferential capital-gains brackets.
- Holding-period count: generally start with the day after acquisition and count
  through the day of sale.

For the 2026 tax year, [Revenue Procedure 2025-32](https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-drop/rp-25-32.pdf)
sets these federal long-term capital-gains thresholds for single filers:

| Capital gains rate | Taxable income layer |
| ------------------ | -------------------- |
| 0%                 | $0 to $49,450        |
| 15%                | $49,451 to $545,500  |
| 20%                | Over $545,500        |

These thresholds look like brackets, but the calculation depends on total
taxable income. Ordinary income fills the lower taxable-income space first; then
long-term capital gains stack on top.

Example for a single filer in 2026:

- ordinary taxable income: $40,000
- net long-term capital gain: $20,000
- first $9,450 of long-term gain fills the remaining space up to $49,450 and is
  taxed at 0%
- remaining $10,550 of long-term gain is taxed at 15% = $1,582.50
- capital-gains tax before credits and other taxes = $1,582.50

Capital losses first offset capital gains. If losses exceed gains, the annual
deduction against ordinary income is limited to $3,000, or $1,500 for Married
Filing Separately; unused loss carries forward.

Watch the exceptions:

- Collectibles and taxable section 1202 qualified small business stock gain can
  be taxed up to 28%.
- Unrecaptured section 1250 real-estate gain can be taxed up to 25%.
- High-income taxpayers may also owe the
  [3.8% net investment income tax](https://www.irs.gov/taxtopics/tc559) on the
  lesser of net investment income or MAGI above the threshold, which is $200,000
  for Single or Head of Household filers and $250,000 for Married Filing Jointly.

## Dividends and Interest Income

[IRS Topic 403](https://www.irs.gov/taxtopics/tc403) says most interest is
taxable in the year it is credited to an account you can withdraw from without
penalty. That includes interest from bank accounts, money market accounts,
certificates of deposit, corporate bonds, tax refunds, and original issue
discount. U.S. Treasury bill, note, and bond interest is taxable federally but
exempt from state and local income tax.

Tax-exempt interest is still reportable. Interest on some state, District of
Columbia, or U.S. territory bonds is reportable but not federally taxable.
Reporting it does not convert it into taxable interest.

[IRS Topic 404](https://www.irs.gov/taxtopics/tc404) separates dividends into
ordinary and qualified dividends:

- Ordinary dividends are included in ordinary income.
- Qualified dividends are ordinary dividends that qualify for lower capital-gain
  tax rates, which makes them behave like long-term capital gains in the tax
  calculation.
- Capital-gain distributions from mutual funds, ETFs, money market funds, and
  REITs are reported as long-term capital gains.
- Return-of-capital distributions are not dividends. They reduce basis first;
  once basis reaches zero, additional nondividend distributions are capital
  gain.

Expect [Form 1099-INT](https://www.irs.gov/taxtopics/tc403) or
[Form 1099-OID](https://www.irs.gov/taxtopics/tc403) when interest or
tax-exempt interest is at least $10, and expect
[Form 1099-DIV](https://www.irs.gov/taxtopics/tc404) when dividends or other
corporate distributions are at least $10. You still report taxable interest and
dividends even if no form arrives.

[Schedule B](https://www.irs.gov/forms-pubs/about-schedule-b-form-1040) is
required in common cases, including when taxable interest or ordinary dividends
exceed $1,500, when you have accrued bond interest, when you are a nominee
recipient, or when the foreign-account questions apply.

Example:

- $2,000 bank interest: ordinary income.
- $600 Treasury interest: ordinary income federally, usually exempt from state
  and local income tax.
- $700 municipal-bond interest: reportable, but not federally taxable if it
  qualifies as tax-exempt interest.
- $1,500 ordinary dividends, of which $1,000 are qualified dividends.

For federal regular tax, this example adds $3,100 of ordinary income: $2,000
bank interest + $600 Treasury interest + $500 nonqualified dividends. The
$1,000 qualified dividend uses the lower qualified-dividend and long-term
capital-gain rate structure. The $700 tax-exempt interest is still reported, but
it is not included in federal taxable income.

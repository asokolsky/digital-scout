# What is Your Price?

Your price is not a moral claim about what you deserve. It is the market price
for your [product](product.md) in a specific [place](place.md): role, level,
industry, company stage, geography, and timing.

Separate three numbers:

- Floor: the minimum offer you would accept.
- Target: the offer that would make you feel fairly paid.
- Ask: the number or range you can say out loud without flinching.

Your ask should be above your target. Your floor should stay private.

## Getting Your Bearings

Use multiple weak signals instead of trusting one source:

- Government data: the BLS Occupational Outlook Handbook and wage data give a
  conservative baseline. For example, BLS reported 2024 median annual pay of
  $133,080 for software developers.
- Salary guides: recruiter salary guides, such as the Motion Recruitment salary
  guide, are useful because they track active hiring markets.
- Public postings: many job postings include salary bands. Save the bands for
  roles that match your level, geography, and industry.
- Network checks: ask people in comparable roles. You will be surprised how much
  people are willing to share if you ask respectfully.
- Offer history: your current and past offers are data, but they are not destiny.

## Anti-pattern

> I am being paid $X but I am worth much more than that, at least $Y, because blah, blah.

Why not to say that: you made your choice and accepted compensation of $X. Hence
you established your going market rate. Fair or not, the market has spoken.

What to do about it: if the effort of finding a new job justifies the potential
gain in compensation, go for it. Nothing personal. Your employer is not your
family. They will replace you faster than you think.

## Full Compensation Package

As an employee, your compensation package may include:

- base salary
- sign-on bonus
- annual or quarterly bonus
- equity compensation:
  - non-qualified stock options (NQSOs)
  - incentive stock options (ISOs)
  - restricted stock units (RSUs)
  - employee stock purchase plan (ESPP)
- healthcare benefits
- retirement account, ideally with employer matching contribution
- other fringe benefits
- support for working from home

The above is a full list. In practice, the particular package rarely includes all
of these items.

When comparing offers, convert everything to expected annual value:

- Cash: base salary plus realistic bonus, not theoretical maximum bonus.
- Sign-on bonus: divide by the clawback period if there is one.
- Public-company equity: annual grant value divided by vesting years, with stock
  price risk noted separately.
- Private-company options: treat as speculative unless you understand the strike
  price, current fair market value, preferred share price, dilution, and
  liquidity path.
- Benefits: include health insurance premiums, employer retirement match, paid
  time off, commute cost, remote-work flexibility, and visa/immigration support
  if relevant.

### Non-qualified Stock Options, NQSOs

NQSOs give you the right to buy company stock at a fixed exercise price. They
are commonly used by private companies and can be granted to employees,
contractors, directors, or advisors.

What to ask:

- How many options are granted?
- What is the strike price?
- What is the current fair market value or 409A valuation?
- What is the vesting schedule?
- How long do you have to exercise after leaving?
- Are early exercise and an 83(b) election available?
- What is the company's fully diluted share count?
- What was the last preferred share price?

Main risk: the option can expire worthless if the company value does not rise
above the strike price, or if you cannot afford to exercise and pay taxes.

Tax note: IRS Topic 427 says nonstatutory stock options without readily
determined market value generally do not create a taxable event at grant, but
income is included when exercised based on the value of the stock received less
the amount paid.

### Incentive Stock Options, ISOs

ISOs are stock options with special tax treatment, but they are available only to
employees and come with more constraints.

What to ask:

- Same questions as NQSOs: share count, strike price, current fair market value,
  vesting, exercise window, fully diluted shares, and last preferred share price.
- Whether the grant qualifies as ISO treatment for the full amount.
- Whether exercise could trigger alternative minimum tax.

Main risk: optimal tax treatment may require holding exercised shares, which
means you can owe tax before you have liquidity. A private-company ISO can
therefore become a real cash-flow problem.

Tax note: IRS Topic 427 says statutory stock options generally are not included
in gross income at grant or exercise, but exercising an ISO may trigger
alternative minimum tax and selling before holding-period requirements can turn
income into ordinary wage income.

### Restricted Stock Units, RSUs

RSUs are a promise to deliver stock or cash after vesting. Public companies often
use RSUs because their value is easier to understand than options: if the shares
vest and the stock is worth anything, the grant has value.

What to ask:

- What is the grant value or share count?
- What is the vesting schedule?
- Are refresh grants typical?
- What happens to unvested RSUs if you leave, retire, are laid off, or the
  company is acquired?
- Is the company public, and can you sell vested shares immediately?

Main risk: you can become over-concentrated in employer stock. If both your job
and your portfolio depend on the same company, you are taking correlated risk.

Tax note: RSUs are generally taxed as compensation when they vest or settle.
After that, any further gain or loss is usually a capital gain or loss.

### Employee Stock Purchase Plan, ESPP

An ESPP lets employees buy company stock, often at a discount, through payroll
deductions. Public companies commonly offer ESPPs.

What to ask:

- What is the discount?
- Is there a lookback provision?
- What are the purchase periods and contribution limits?
- Can you sell immediately after purchase?
- What happens if you leave before the purchase date?

Main risk: the discount can be attractive, but holding the shares afterward
adds employer-stock concentration risk.

Tax note: IRS Topic 427 treats options granted under an employee stock purchase
plan as statutory stock options and points to Form 3922 for reporting stock
acquired under an ESPP.

## Offer Comparison Checklist

Before accepting an offer, write down:

- base salary
- target bonus and how often it actually pays out
- sign-on bonus and clawback terms
- equity type, vesting schedule, and liquidity risk
- healthcare premium and deductible
- 401(k) match and vesting schedule
- paid time off and holiday policy
- remote-work expectations
- commute cost and expected office days
- severance policy if available
- immigration or relocation support if relevant

Then compare offers using total expected annual value, not just base salary.

## Communicating your Price

Common recommendations:

- Stick to ranges: "For this role and scope, I am targeting total compensation
  in the $X to $Y range."
- Anchor to scope: "That depends on level, equity, bonus structure, and remote
  expectations."
- Postpone when possible: "I would like to understand the role and leveling
  first, then we can make sure the compensation range is aligned."
- Do not volunteer your floor.
- Do not negotiate from need. Negotiate from market evidence and the value of the
  role.
- The more junior you are, the less negotiation power you hold, but you should
  still ask.

If the offer is close:

> I am excited about the role. If you can move the offer to $X base, I am ready
> to accept.

If the company cannot move base salary:

> Is there flexibility on sign-on bonus, equity, remote schedule, or review
> timing?
